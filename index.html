Analyzing NFL Play Data¶

Jake Richard, CMSC320 Final Project¶

Since the NFL was formed in 1920, the game of football has changed a lot. Teams in the NFL who are successful not only have the most talented and well-trained athletes, but also have great coaches who innovate the game with new strategies on offense and defense. Over time, different styles of offense and defense have emerged dominant in different stages of the game.

In the early life of the NFL, handing the ball off to the running back was the primary option on offense. Teams then began to develop a passing game. The quarterback's role became more and more important, and teams with the right quaterback and wide receivers were able to win. Many teams didn't know the best ways to defend against the pass.

In today's NFL, teams are built around franchise quarterbacks, with pass heavy offenses complemented by a steady ground game in order to always keep the defense on it's toes.

When most of the professional sports leagues started keeping statistics on everything about the game, coaches and players began to gain more insight on various game situations. In every play, there's a decision to be made about what play your offense/defense is going to execute, and many more factors that determine the outcome of that play.

In this tutorial, I will analyze play data from 2009 - 2017, and look for some interesting insights I can find in four areas:
Total Yardage
Run vs. Pass

In fact, we can model the conversion chance based on how many yards a team as left to the first down marker

In [12]:
FourthDownPlays = RunsAndPasses[RunsAndPasses["down"] == 4]
FourthDownPlays = FourthDownPlays[["PlayType", "posteam", "ydstogo", "Yards.Gained", "desc"]]

def calcPercentConvert(ydstogo):
    FourthDownydstogo = FourthDownPlays[FourthDownPlays["ydstogo"] > ydstogo]
    
    FourthDownFails = FourthDownydstogo[FourthDownydstogo["Yards.Gained"] < FourthDownydstogo["ydstogo"]]
    FourthDownWins = FourthDownydstogo[FourthDownydstogo["Yards.Gained"] >= FourthDownydstogo["ydstogo"]]

    numFails = FourthDownFails.shape[0]
    numWins = FourthDownWins.shape[0]
    
    if (numWins+numFails != 0):
        percentConvert = numWins/(numFails+numWins)
    else: 
        percentConvert = 0
    return percentConvert

ydstogoConvert = pd.DataFrame(columns=["ydstogo", "PercentConvert"], dtype='float')
for i in range(0,28):
    newdf = pd.DataFrame([[i + 1, calcPercentConvert(i+1)]], columns=["ydstogo", "PercentConvert"])
    ydstogoConvert = ydstogoConvert.append(newdf)
    
ydstogoConvert = ydstogoConvert.reset_index()
sns.scatterplot("ydstogo", "PercentConvert", data=ydstogoConvert)

Field Goals vs. Extra points¶

Prior to the 2015 season, when an extra point was attempted, the ball was snapped at the two yard line, which led to about a 19 yard extra point kick. At the start of the 2015 season, the extra point attempt snap was moved back to the 15 yard line, which created a 33 yard extra point kick. Inituitively, this move back would seem to lower the percentage of completed extra points, but lets look at the numbers.

This begs the question, how does the distance impact the field goal completion percentage? We can create code similar to our fourth down calculations and graph the percentages against the distance.

The closest field goal we can take is around 18-19 yards (10 for the end zone, 7 for the snap, and 1 or 2 for the distance from the goal line). The longest field goal that has been completed in regulation is 63 yards, but in recent history the longest is 62. So we'll work with distances 19-62 yards out

def calcFGPercentConvert(distance):
    FieldGoalsDist = FieldGoals[(FieldGoals["FieldGoalDistance"] == distance)]
    
    FieldGoalsMade = FieldGoalsDist[FieldGoalsDist["FieldGoalResult"] == "Good"]
    FieldGoalsMissed = FieldGoalsDist[FieldGoalsDist["FieldGoalResult"] == "No Good"]
    
    Made = FieldGoalsMade.shape[0]
    Missed = FieldGoalsMissed.shape[0]
    
    if (Made+Missed != 0):
        percentConvert = Made/(Made+Missed)
    else: 
        percentConvert = 0
    return percentConvert

Conclusions¶

Throughout this project, we've looked at a lot of different statistics related to football.

From our yardage data, I think that it would have been interesting to see data from more seasons than I have. If I was able to find yardage data from a coulpe decades back, we would have some more interesting yardage calculations. A lot of NFL data (Besides win-loss data) is fairly new, and so it's tough to track total yardage with just the 9 years of data we have.

Viewing the data from a coach's perspective, there were a few interesting takeaways from my data analysis. First, passing the ball on first down is not a bad option. I think there are balances into establishing a good run game, but there is some merit to starting a series off with some high yards chances as shown by the data.

In fourth down situations, It makes sense to have an awareness of the amount of yards to convert, because starting out in a fourth down situation a coach is already facing a less than 50% completion percentage rate. As soon as we hit about 10 yards to go for the first down, our chances have nearly halved. But after about 15-20 yards, it's basically a toss up at that point, and you need to be pretty desperate to try and convert with that much room to go. Most conversions successful at the distance are due to great chance plays or poor defensive errors.

I think it's interesting that field goals from similar distances to extra points are made less. I think it mainly has to do with the higher pressure of field goal situations in games that cause kickers to slip up. The push back of the extra point has been a little but controversial, but overall it's been working in making the scoring more variable and we can see the impacts that this is having in the stats. When it comes to attempting field goals, I would advise anything under 40 yards to be a pretty safe bet. We slowly decrease 50% field goal completion rate as the distance approaches about 55 yards. Kicks from 60+ yards out are fairly unlikely, so I'd tread carefully when thinking about taking kicks from that distance.

Overall, It comes down to the ability of the players and coaches to work together and make the best decisions for their teams and execute those plays to help their team succeed. These statistics can help coaches and players understand how their role on and off the field impacts the game in every situation.
